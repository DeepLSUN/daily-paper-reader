# A Theoretical Interpretation of In-Context Learning via Probabilistic Modeling 

Zhenyu Liu, Huaze Tang, and Shao-Lun Huang 

Abstract —In-context learning (ICL) is an emerging paradigm that employs the semantic information inherent in large language models (LLMs) for generating answers to user queries. While the remarkable performance of ICL has been widely known, a general modeling and a rigorous theoretical analysis of this paradigm are still lacking. This work presents a probabilistic model for ICL and derives the performance of ICL for both general parametric distributions and exponential families. Based on the derived results, the work explains the impact of multiple factors such as the number of demonstrations, the sensitivity of the probabilistic model to the variation of its parameters, as well as the similarity between the demonstrations and the query on the performance of ICL. 

Index Terms —In-context learning, large language models, Fisher information, KL divergence, exponential family 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Large language models (LLMs) has been successfully ap-plied in different research fields including communication, sensing, and optimization [1]–[3]. In-context learning (ICL) is a promising paradigm for learning built upon the capability of LLMs [4]. In ICL, an LLM generates an answer to a user query based on a few demonstrations provided beforehand via the context prompt. Specifically, ICL exploits the semantic knowledge inherent in LLMs for responding to user queries without changing the pre-trained LLM parameters. As a result, ICL can achieve desirable performance using only a few demonstrations without significant computational overhead. The remarkable performance of ICL has piqued signif-icant research interest in understanding its mechanism via theoretical analysis. In particular, recent studies have pro-posed multiple explanations for the mechanism of ICL. One prominent line of research interprets ICL through the lens of implicit meta-learning or Bayesian inference. In this view, the prompt implies a latent task, and transformers adapt by approximating Bayesian model averaging over the space of potential tasks [5]–[8]. Complementary studies emphasize an algorithmic perspective, demonstrating that transformers can simulate standard estimation algorithms. For instance, it has been shown that the attention mechanism can approximate gradient descent updates, with deeper layers effectively per-forming iterative optimization steps [9]–[12].

Another perspective highlights the critical role of pre-training data distribution. Research suggests that sufficiently broad task coverage and diversity during pre-training are essential for the emergence of robust in-context generaliza-tion [13], [14]. Conversely, narrow pre-training may lead to biased predictors that fail to generalize to new tasks. This connects to the practical challenge of prompt engineering, where performance is highly sensitive to the selection and ordering of demonstrations. While various retrieval-based and heuristic strategies have been proposed to select effective demonstrations [15]–[17], a rigorous theoretical understanding of why certain demonstrations are more effective than others remains limited. To address the complexity of practical LLMs and bridge the gap between theory and practice, [18] proposed a linear probabilistic model for the conditional distribution of the answer given the input text. Based on this model, the work develops a theory to explain the effects of multiple factors on the ICL performance. This theory, while providing significant interpretation of ICL, still has a gap with practice as the linear probabilistic model may not fit with existing LLMs. In this work, we propose a probabilistic model for ICL, analyze the performance of ICL based on this model, and present interpretations of the theoretical analysis. In particular, an input text and its corresponding answer are both modeled as random variables. The LLM uses the demonstrations to learn the conditional distribution of the answer given the input text. We adopt the expected excessive risk (EER), namely the expected Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence between the ground-truth conditional distribution and the one inferred by the LLM, as the performance metric of ICL. We derive the asymptotic EER for both general parametric distributions and exponential families, and establish a non-asymptotic upper bound of EER for exponential families. Building on these results, we explain the effect of the following factors on ICL: the number of demonstrations, the sensitivity of the conditional distribution to the variation of parameters, and the similarity between the demonstrations and the query. In this work, we propose a probabilistic model for ICL and analyze its EER. For general parametric distributions, we show that the asymptotic EER is the ratio between the EER coefficient and the number of demonstrations. For exponential families, the EER coefficient is a function of the FIM at the quest and of the FIM averaged over all the demonstra-tions. Moreover, a non-asymptotic upper bound on the EER is derived for exponential families. Based on these results, we show the effect on ICL performance of different factors including the number of demonstrations, the sensitivity of the probabilistic model to the variation of its parameters, as well as the similarity between the demonstrations and the query. This work sheds theoretical insights on ICL and provides design guidelines for model training and prompt engineering in LLM. APPENDIX

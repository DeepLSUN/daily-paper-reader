# HermesHFL: Incentive-Compatible Hierarchical Federated Unlearning for Dynamic LLM Fine-Tuning 

Chenxi Sun, Student Member, IEEE , Minghui Liwang, Senior Member, IEEE , Wusi He, Zhang Liu, Yuhan Su, Seyyedali Hosseinalipour, Senior Member, IEEE , Xianbin Wang, Fellow, IEEE, Yiguang Hong, Fellow, IEEE 

✦

Abstract —Hierarchical federated unlearning (HFUL) for large language model (LLM) fine-tuning introduces fundamental challenges stemming from hierarchical aggregation dependencies, dynamic client participa-tion, and the strong parameter coupling inherent to LLM adaptation. In particular, selectively removing the influence of participating clients be-comes substantially more difficult in HFUL, where model updates propa-gate through multiple aggregation stages and client unlearning requests may occur concurrently with client departures and subsequent rejoin-ing behaviors. To address these challenges, we propose HermesHFL (hierarchical rejoinable machine learning with selective unlearning for incentive-compatible HFL), a hierarchical rejoinable federated learning framework with selective unlearning for incentive-compatible LLM fine-tuning. HermesHFL supports dynamic client participation, selective un-learning, and client reintegration while enabling scalable LLM adaptation through parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) with LoRA. We formulate a unified optimization problem that jointly captures client participation, edge association, incentive allocation, and unlearning decisions under heterogeneous and strategic client behaviors. To efficiently solve this challenging problem, we develop Neogen (neural network guided dual network evolutionary optimization), a neural-guided bilevel evolutionary optimization framework that combines covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) for continuous incentive optimization with cross-generational elitist selection, heterogeneous recombination, and cataclysmic mutation evolutionary algorithm (CHC)-based mechanism for discrete client participation and association decisions. A neural sur-rogate guidance mechanism is further introduced to accelerate conver-gence and reduce search complexity. Extensive experiments on LLM fine-tuning tasks demonstrate that HermesHFL consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in terms of model utility, unlearning effective-ness, convergence stability, and resource efficiency. 

Index Terms —Hierarchical Federated Learning, Machine Unlearning, Large Language Models, LoRA, Incentive Mechanism, Dynamic Clients

# THE proliferation of smart devices has led to mas-sive data generation across the Internet-of-Things (IoT) ecosystem, creating unprecedented opportunities for ma-chine learning (ML). However, increasingly stringent pri-vacy regulations and data-governance constraints make conventional centralized ML pipelines impractical. This challenge has driven the emergence of distributed learning paradigms, among which federated learning (FL) [1] stands out as a representative framework for privacy-preserving collaborative model training. Despite its advantages, con-ventional FL suffers from heavy communication overhead, aggregation latency, and unstable convergence in large-scale, heterogeneous networks. To address these limitations, hierarchical federated learning (HFL) [2] introduces a multi-tier aggregation architecture, where intermediate aggrega-tors (e.g., edge servers) perform local model aggregations prior to global model aggregation. Meanwhile, the rise of large language models (LLMs) [3] pushes distributed learning into a far more demanding regime characterized by extreme model scale, high computational cost, and strin-gent requirements on communication efficiency and train-ing stability. Although HFL predates LLMs, its hierarchical structure naturally aligns with their training/fine-tuning demands [4]. At the same time, the substantial parameter coupling and resource demands of LLMs intensify chal-lenges in coordination, efficiency, robustness, and privacy under dynamic client participation, necessitating more re-fined layer-aware designs and aggregation strategies. These emerging challenges raise fundamental research questions (RQs), motivating this study. 

1.1 Core Motivation 

Addressing the following RQs forms the central motivation of this paper. 

RQ 1: With increasing privacy protection demands, how can the influence of specific clients or data be effectively removed from LLMs in HFL architecture? Driven by privacy regula-tions such as the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which establishes the right to erasure , the ability to remove the influence of specific clients/data from trained arXiv:2607.11528v1 [cs.CE] 13 Jul 2026 2

models, known as federated unlearning [5], has attracted increasing attention. However, enabling unlearning in HFL for LLM fine-tuning is considerably more challenging due to the large scale and strong parameter coupling inherent to LLMs, the complex dependencies introduced by hierarchical multi-stage aggregation, and the need to remove targeted knowledge while preserving overall model utility and train-ing stability. Moreover, unlearning requests typically occur after fine-tuning has partially or fully converged, making retraining from scratch computationally and communica-tionally prohibitive, especially for LLMs. Consequently, effi-cient unlearning methods are needed to substantially reduce the unlearning overhead of LLMs, while preserving model utility in HFL settings. 

RQ 2: What principled cross-layer optimization and incentive design can govern unlearning for LLMs in HFL, so that clients with erasure decisions are jointly coordinated with provable guarantees on utility, stability, and resource efficiency under strategic and dynamic participation? HFL with unlearning naturally entails working with a cross-layer, multi-agent system in which global, intermediate, and client nodes interact through coupled update-erasure dynamics. In this setting, heterogeneous objectives, costs, and privacy pref-erences induce strategic client behavior, leading to free-riding, misreporting, and unstable participation that un-dermine efficiency and reliability. Effective coordination therefore requires addressing (i) participant selection and edge association, (ii) contribution measurement and credit assignment across hierarchical aggregation paths, and (iii) 

reward/penalty allocation that is incentive-compatible and budget-feasible. Moreover, unlearning requires the influence of designated participants to be removed efficiently and ver-ifiably without disrupting the ongoing fine-tuning process, where fine-tuning stability and hierarchical aggregation consistency are tightly coupled, removing influential par-ticipants may propagate perturbations across aggregation stages, potentially degrading model utility, slowing conver-gence, and increasing training variance. Hence, scalable and stable unlearning in LLM-oriented HFL systems demands incentive-compatible, cross-layer coordinated mechanisms. 

RQ 3: What is the role of client rejoining following unlearning in HFL, and how can such dynamic re-participation be enabled in a secure, efficient, and stability-preserving manner? In prac-tical distributed learning systems, client participation and privacy requirements evolve over time due to contextual, regulatory, and user-specific factors. A client may request unlearning and temporarily withdraw from training when privacy concerns intensify, then later rejoin the learning process once those concerns diminish or regulatory con-straints expire. For example, in smart healthcare applica-tions, patient records may require strict privacy protection during diagnosis and treatment phases but become less sen-sitive after recovery or beyond mandated retention periods. Consequently, supporting leave–unlearn–rejoin dynamics is both practically relevant and important for sustaining long-term model utility and participant engagement. 1.2 Novelty and Contributions 

To enable effective LLM fine-tuning over dynamic and het-erogeneous HFL environments with selective unlearning, we propose hierarchical rejoinable machine learning with selective unlearning for incentive-compatible HFL (abbrevi-ated as HermesHFL, depicted in Fig. 1), a novel framework that explicitly models the leave–unlearn–rejoin lifecycle of clients while incorporating economic incentive mechanisms to govern dynamic learning and unlearning behaviors in LLM-oriented HFL systems. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows: 

• HFL with Rejoinable Unlearning for LLMs: We develop one of the first resource-efficient, privacy-preserving, and mutually-beneficial hierarchical federated unlearn-ing (HFUL) frameworks that supports dynamic client participation, selective unlearning, and client rejoining for LLM fine-tuning. In particular, to the best of our knowledge, this study is among the first efforts to rigor-ously formalize the leave-unlearn-rejoin lifecycle within dynamic LLM-oriented HFL systems with incentives. 

• President-Manager-Worker Architecture with Incentive-Aware Optimization: We form a novel multi-layer archi-tecture that encompasses multiple players: (i) a cloud server as the ’president’ with LLM fine-tuning task and a budget pool; (ii) several geographically-distributed edge servers as ’managers’, responsible for client se-lection/association with incentives; and (iii) multiple clients as ’workers’, helping with model fine-tuning, which may have unlearn/rejoin requests. We then for-mulate a multi-objective optimization problem, captur-ing the interactions among different players, with the 3

aim of meeting the model fine-tuning demand (e.g., accuracy) and mutual benefits, under key constraints such as budget.  

> •

Dual-Level Evolutionary Optimization Guided by Neu-ral Networks: We develop Neogen (depicted in Fig. 1), which adopts a hierarchical bilevel architecture, where the upper level optimizes incentive allocation and budget planning through a customized CMA-ES (covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy) with adaptive covariance control, while the lower level resolves client participation, association, and un-learning decisions via a problem-specific CHC (cross-generational elitist selection, heterogeneous recombina-tion, and cataclysmic mutation) evolutionary mecha-nism. The two levels are coordinated through an iter-ative bilevel optimization scheme, further accelerated by neural network-guided surrogate evaluation to re-duce search complexity. This design enables stable and incentive-compatible decision making while improving resource efficiency and convergence stability in dy-namic LLM-oriented HFUL systems.  

> •

Experimental Validation: Extensive experiments on LLM fine-tuning across diverse datasets and models, upon testing dynamic and heterogeneous settings demon-strate notable improvements achieved by HermesHFL over state-of-the-art benchmark methods in terms of model utility, unlearning effectiveness, convergence speed, and resource efficiency. 

2 LITERATURE REVIEW 

In the following, we review the most relevant existing studies and highlight their key distinctions from our work. 

• Federated Unlearning: Federated unlearning (FUL), e.g., class-discriminative pruning [6], seeks to remove the influ-ence of designated clients or data from trained models in FL systems. Existing FUL methods are primarily developed for flat FL (i.e., networks with direct client-to-server communi-cations) and rely on gradient ascent (GA), influence func-tions, differential privacy, or parameter resetting. For exam-ple, FedOSD [7] introduces unlearning cross-entropy loss with steepest descent to mitigate gradient conflicts while preserving model performance. FedRecovery [8] removes weighted gradient residuals under differential privacy, pro-ducing models statistically close to retraining without full retraining. FRAMU [9] combines attention-based unlearning with federated reinforcement learning to support continual model evolution. F2UL [10] considers fairness by assessing label-free model quality and adaptively allocating models to reduce the impact of low-quality data in FUL. For LLM-oriented unlearning, GRU [11] modifies gradient update strategies to reduce interference between the retained and removed knowledge during unlearning, whereas DSMGDA [12] formulates unlearning as a multi-objective optimization problem to prevent gradient explosion. To reduce retraining cost, KNOT [13] employs clustering-based aggregation to localize unlearning operations, significantly lowering the overall computational burden. While these studies advance the efficiency of FUL, they are mostly designed for flat FL and also do not consider leave-unlearn-rejoin lifecycle over dynamic clients in hierarchical architectures. Moreover, the above works rarely integrate incentives with privacy-preserving unlearning, leaving critical gaps in consistency and sustainability for LLM-oriented HFUL systems. 

• Parameter-Efficient LLM Fine-tuning via FL: Full train-ing of LLMs is computationally prohibitive in resource-constrained and heterogeneous FL environments, mak-ing parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) a dominant approach. Frameworks, such as FATE-LLM [14], enable industrial-scale federated LLM fine-tuning by combining local PEFT updates with privacy protection mechanisms, effectively reducing both computational and communica-tion costs. Also, FedALoRA [15] combines low-rank adap-tation (LoRA) technique with personalized aggregation to accommodate cross-domain non-independent and identi-cally distributed (Non-IID) data across clients. FedBiOT [16] employs an emulator–adapter structure for two-layer optimization, enabling domain-specific learning without re-quiring access to the full LLM model. FLORA [17] addresses aggregation biases in heterogeneous LoRA configurations through stacked aggregation strategies. LoRA-FAIR [18] in-troduces correction terms to mitigate server-side aggrega-tion bias and inconsistent initialization across clients. PAC+ [19] integrates parallel adapters, activation caching and distributed collaborative training to achieve efficient LLM fine-tuning with significantly reduced memory footprint. MIRA [20] enables personalized LLM optimization, while HRL-FLLM [21] adaptively optimizes LLM fine-tuning in edge networks to reduce computing/communication over-head and training latency while ensuring model conver-gence. FedARA [22] employs truncated SVD adaptation, dynamic rank allocation, and rank-based module pruning to optimize model performance, communication efficiency, and resource consumption on edge devices. Despite their notable advancements, the above works on PEFT-based FL are generally confined to flat FL, neglecting cross-layer consistency challenges of hierarchical aggregation in HFL setups. Also, they overlook selective unlearning operations that preserve LLM utility while responding to dynamic client rejoin requests in HFUL. 

• Incentive Design Mechanisms for FUL. To ensure sustainable participation in FL/FUL systems, it is important to incorpo-rate economic incentive mechanisms, where compensation plays a central role in motivating rational/strategic clients and encouraging reliable data contribution and long-term participation. In this regard, early study [23] introduced context-aware dynamic budget allocation to attract high-quality participants for FL, while mitigating reward imbal-ance and communication latency disparities. Another work [24] integrated clustering-based FUL with incentives, where unlearning tasks are executed within client clusters and participation strategies are dynamically optimized using evolutionary game theory and deep reinforcement learning. Studies [25] and [26] applied principal-agent theory and reverse game design to enforce verifiable and incentive-compatible client behavior. Despite their notable contribu-tions, these studies have not explored designing incentive mechanisms for HFUL in dynamic environments with client rejoining, an effort carried out for the first time in this work. 4

3 PRELIMINARIES 

In the following, we introduce the key concepts considered in this work, namely LoRA, HFL, and machine unlearning. 

• LoRA: The core idea of LoRA is to constrain model weight updates ∆ω on a given model ω0 ∈ Rd×k through low-rank decomposition in the form of: 

ω0 + ∆ ω = ω0 + BA . (1) Specifically, instead of training the entire weight matrix, LoRA only updates the parameter set composed of B ∈

Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k, where r denotes the adaptation rank controlling the expressive capacity of the update. To pre-serve the behavior of the pretrained model at initialization while maintaining stable gradient propagation during back propagation, matrix A is typically initialized using Gaussian random values, whereas matrix B is initialized to zero. Moreover, the low-rank update is commonly scaled by a predefined factor to stabilize optimization. The choice of rank r plays a critical role in balancing adaptation capability, computational efficiency, and communication overhead. 

• HFL: In HFL, let g, e, and l denote the indices of the global aggregation round, edge-level aggregation round, and lo-cal training iteration, respectively. Under this hierarchical training paradigm, clients perform multiple local training iterations and transmit their updated model parameters to intermediate edge aggregators for regional aggregation. The aggregated edge models are subsequently forwarded to the central server for global aggregation. The corresponding aggregation operations can be compactly written as follows:

4 OVERVIEW AND BASIC MODELING 

4.1 Overview 

We consider an HFUL system with economic incentives and dynamic client leave–unlearn–rejoin behaviors to support efficient and privacy-preserving LLM fine-tuning. The con-sidered system consists of three main entities/players: (i) 

a cloud server responsible for global aggregation, referred to as the president ; (ii) a set of geographically distributed edge aggregators, referred to as managers , represented by 

M = {m1, ..., m j , ..., m |M |}; and (iii) a set of distributed clients participating in local model training, referred to as 

workers , represented by W = {w1, ..., w i, ..., w |W |}. To facil-itate LLM fine-tuning and unlearning over such a market, we propose HermesHFL, a cross-layer optimization frame-work integrating participation control, aggregation policies, incentives, and selective unlearning. In HermesHFL, client joining, withdrawal, unlearning, and rejoining behaviors induce tightly coupled system dynamics that simultane-ously affect worker-level decisions, manager-side aggrega-tion, and global coordination at the president. The overall operation of HermesHFL consists of iterative global aggre-gation rounds, each involving local training, edge aggre-gation, incentive coordination, and selective unlearning. Its details are provided in the subsequent discussions. 

• Step 1. Global Model Downloading and Budget Distribution: 

Prior to the g-th global aggregation round, the president distributes the latest global model to all managers 1, while 

1. All managers receive the global model from the president, while only selected workers download the model from associated managers. 5

simultaneously allocating the total incentive budget across managers to attract high-quality workers for collaborative fine-tuning. 

• Step 2. Manager-Worker Contracting, Local Fine-Tuning, and Edge Aggregation: Managers establish contracts with work-ers, specifying incentive payments, target training quality, and potential unlearning penalties according to system and worker characteristics. Subsequently, workers perform lo-cal LLM fine-tuning using the LoRA PEFT technique. The resulting LoRA parameters are uploaded to the associated managers. Each manager then performs multiple edge-level aggregation rounds over the collected updates over time and forwards the aggregated model to the president for global aggregation, thereby completing one global round. 

• Step 3. Unlearning Process: Upon completion of a global aggregation round, participating workers receive payments as stipulated in contracts, while some workers may sub-sequently request unlearning (e.g., due to market exit or privacy concerns). In such cases, they perform GA during subsequent training and compensate managers according to the agreed liquidated damages, which are incorporated into the managers’ total budget for the (g + 1) -th round. After several unlearning iterations, completion is verified via the Kullback–Leibler divergence (KLD) between the model’s predictive distributions on the unlearned worker’s data and a reference dataset. At this time, some information of the worker (computing capacity, communication capability, privacy cost, and reputation score, e.g., estimated via a sliding time window reflecting the unlearning probability) is updated. Specifically, workers are allowed to rejoin the market after completing the unlearning process 2. The above process repeats until reaching a predefined number of global rounds. In the following, we introduce the basic modeling com-ponents of HermesHFL, including the contract formulation, worker attributes, and budget modeling for the president and managers.

Problem Transformation: The original problem P(g)0 consti-tutes a three-layer hierarchical optimization involving the president, managers, and workers. The problem is particu-larly challenging due to the tight coupling among hetero-geneous decision variables across different layers, includ-ing binary worker-selection variables, continuous budget-allocation variables, and non-linear utility-maximization variables. These are mutually dependent, where upper-layer budget allocation directly affects lower-layer coalition formation and incentive assignment, while lower-layer deci-sions in turn influence the overall system utility. Such cross-layer interactions lead to a highly coupled mixed-integer nonlinear optimization problem with a combinatorial deci-sion space and non-convex objective structure. Furthermore, the objectives of the president and managers are intrin-sically aligned, as both aim to maximize model-training 9

utility under limited budget resources. 6 PROBLEM SOLUTION DESIGN METHODOLOGY 

We propose NEural netwOrk Guided dual network Evolutionary optimizatioN (Neogen) to address the two layer optimization problem P(g)1 . Specifically, Neogen decomposes the opti-mization process into two interconnected levels. The up-per level determines u(g) = {p(g) 

> i,j

, q (g) 

> i,j

}, which correspond to the incentive payments and unlearning compensations associated with worker–manager pairs. Meanwhile, the lower level determines v(g) = {x(g) 

> i

, y (g) 

> i,j

}, which represent worker selection and worker–manager association decisions made by managers in response to workers’ proposals. In particular, this integrates two complementary evolutionary optimization mechanisms together with a neural network (NN)-based guidance module: (i) covariance matrix adap-tation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) at the upper level, and 

(ii) a cross-generational elitist selection, heterogeneous re-combination, and cataclysmic mutation (CHC) evolutionary algorithm at the lower level. This hybrid design is motivated by the mixed continuous-binary nature of the optimization variables and the need to efficiently solve the resulting bilevel optimization problem. Specifically, at the upper level, we adopt CMA-ES for the continuous optimization of in-centive variables. Since the objective function depends on the solution of the lower-level combinatorial problem, no explicit gradient information is available. CMA-ES is par-ticularly well-suited here, owing to its covariance adapta-tion capability and strong robustness in highly non-convex search spaces. However, the lower-level optimization vari-ables are binary, resulting in a combinatorial search space in which CMA-ES is not directly applicable without relaxation 10 

or specialized binary encoding. To address this at the lower level, we adopt the CHC evolutionary algorithm, which is specifically designed for binary optimization and provides strong diversity preservation and global exploration ability through Hamming distance-based mating control and cata-clysmic restart mechanisms. We note that, solving the lower-level optimization prob-lem for every candidate upper-level solution introduces substantial computational overhead. To alleviate this issue, Neogen further incorporates an NN-based guidance to ap-proximate the implicit mapping v∗ = Φ( u), which maps upper-level decisions to their corresponding lower-level optimal solutions. Specifically, during the early stages of op-timization, exact lower-level solutions obtained via CHC are used to train the neural network. Once the NN achieves suf-ficient approximation accuracy, it predicts promising lower-level solutions for newly generated upper-level candidates. These predicted solutions are subsequently refined within a restricted Hamming neighborhood through CHC-based local evolutionary search. By initializing the lower-level search around NN-predicted regions, Neogen effectively reduces the lower-level search space while preserving the exploration and refinement abilities of evolutionary opti-mization. Hereafter, we introduce the major components of Neogen.

8 CONCLUSION 

This paper presents HermesHFL, an incentive-compatible hierarchical federated unlearning framework for dynamic client leave–unlearn–rejoin LLM LoRA fine-tuning. We con-struct a cross-layer market optimization model to jointly formulate incentive contracts, client scheduling, edge asso-ciation and verifiable gradient-ascent unlearning comply-ing with GDPR erasure rules. To resolve the non-convex mixed-integer bilevel optimization arising from hierarchical aggregation and strategic clients, we develop Neogen, a neural surrogate-aided dual evolutionary solver that unites CMA-ES for continuous incentive tuning and CHC for dis-crete client assignment, greatly reducing search costs and stabilizing convergence. Extensive GPT-2 experiments on SST-2 and AGNews verify HermesHFL’s superiority over federated learning baselines in generalization, unlearning fidelity, multi-agent utility and computation efficiency. Our Rejoin strategy delivers competitive accuracy against full retraining while drastically shortening runtime, restoring model performance within only 1–2 global rounds after unlearning requests. 16 

For future research, several promising directions remain to be explored. Extending HermesHFL to multi-modal LLMs and asynchronous hierarchical aggregation pipelines to sup-port real-time streaming client data, as well as integrat-ing differential privacy into the gradient-ascent unlearning workflow to provide rigorous privacy guarantees for client data erasure, represent valuable research avenues.

APPENDIX AILLUSTRATIVE EXAMPLES OF ALIGNED OBJEC -

TIVES 

The transformation from the original three-layer optimiza-tion problem to a two-layer framework is motivated by the observation that upper-level and middle-level decision makers often pursue highly aligned objectives in hierarchi-cal systems. 

Example 1: Financial Resource Allocation. In a finan-cial institution, a headquarters allocates investment budgets across multiple regional branches to maximize overall re-turns under limited capital resources. Each branch further distributes its allocated budget among local projects to im-prove regional investment performance. Although decision making occurs at different levels, both headquarters and branches aim to maximize the same system-level utility (overall financial return), differing only in the granularity of their decisions. 

Example 2: Healthcare Resource Management. In a hospital, administrators allocate medical resources such as staff, equipment, and treatment capacity across different departments to maximize overall healthcare quality. Each department then optimizes local treatment assignments to patients under the allocated resources. Again, although the hospital administration and individual departments operate at different levels, their objectives are aligned: improving overall healthcare outcomes under resource constraints. 17 

Inspired by such hierarchical systems, this work em-beds the president’s objective into the manager-level opti-mization through the contribution-based budget allocation mechanism defined in (32). This transformation preserves the original optimization preference while eliminating an explicit upper-level optimization layer, thereby reducing computational complexity.

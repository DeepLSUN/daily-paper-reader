Abstract 

This paper describes the first ChineseBabyLM challenge, which will be held in the 2026 NLPCC conference. The challenge calls for researchers to train language models from scratch with 100 million Chinese tokens and evaluates the models on 3 tracks of tasks: NLU, cognitive alignment and Hanzi knowledge. There is no restriction on tokenizer, model architecture and the number of training epochs. Details of the challenge can be found in https://chinese-babylm. github.io/ .

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models have achieved strong performance across many language understanding tasks, but their success has typically depended on pretraining with very large corpora and substantial compute. This data-hungry paradigm contrasts sharply with human language acquisition: children acquire robust linguistic competence from comparatively limited, naturalistic input. The BabyLM Challenge was created to make this contrast experimentally useful by asking participants to train language models under developmentally motivated data budgets, encouraging research on sample-efficient pretraining, cognitively plausible data, and evaluation protocols that go beyond raw scale (Warstadt et al., 2023b). The original BabyLM shared tasks focused primarily on English. Recent multilingual efforts such as BabyBabelLM extend the same motivation to a wider set of languages, showing that data-efficient language modeling should be studied under typologically and orthographically diverse conditions (Jumelet et al., 2026). Chinese is an especially important test case. It lacks explicit word boundaries, makes extensive use of compounding, permits flexible syntactic configurations, and uses a logographic writing system in which characters carry visual, structural, and phonological regularities. These properties make Chinese language learning a poor fit for evaluation protocols designed only around alphabetic, whitespace-delimited languages, and they raise distinct questions about tokenization, character-level representation, and the amount of data needed to acquire linguistic generalizations. The ChineseBabyLM Challenge is a shared task for studying data-efficient and cognitively plausible language modeling for Chinese. Participating systems must be pretrained from randomly initialized weights, either on the official 102-million-word corpus derived from the Chinese portion of BabyBabelLM (Jumelet et al., 2026) or on a self-compiled corpus within the same word budget. This setup preserves the central BabyLM constraint while allowing participants to explore different model architectures, tokenizers, data selection strategies, and training methods for Chinese. The challenge evaluates models along three complementary dimensions. The Natural Language Understanding track combines zero-shot minimal-pair evaluation, including ZhoBLiMP (Liu et al., 2026), with supervised Chinese understand-ing tasks from CLUE (Xu et al., 2020). The Hanzi track directly tests whether models acquire structural and phonological knowledge about Chinese characters from limited input. The Cognitive Modeling track uses Chinese fMRI benchmarks from MulCogBench (Zhang et al., 2025) to measure how well model representations predict human neural responses during word- and discourse-level language comprehension. Together, these tracks are intended to reward models that are not only accurate on downstream tasks, but also sensitive to Chinese-specific linguistic structure and aligned with cognitively motivated evaluation criteria. This paper describes the design of the first ChineseBabyLM Challenge, including the training rules, evaluation tracks, task inventory, baseline systems, and initial results. By providing a standardized corpus, an open evaluation pipeline, hidden final-evaluation tasks, and reproducible baselines, the shared task aims to support a research community around Chinese data-efficient language modeling and to contribute a language-specific counterpart to the broader BabyLM pro-gram. 1arXiv:2607.10745v1 [cs.CL] 12 Jul 2026 2 Evaluation Tasks 

The ChineseBabyLM Challenge evaluates data-efficient Chinese language models along three complementary dimensions of linguistic competence: natural language understanding, character-level knowledge, and cognitive plausibility. The final evaluation consists of 14 tasks grouped into three tracks: eight Natural Language Understanding (NLU) tasks, four Hanzi tasks, and two Cognitive Modeling tasks. 

Evaluation formats. The tasks use three evaluation formats. First, the zero-shot minimal-pair tasks evaluate whether a model assigns a higher score to a well-formed or semantically plausible sentence x+ than to a minimally different ill-formed or implausible sentence x−. For the Hanzi tasks, if the target character or character components are not recognized by the tokenizer (UNK), then the model is considered to fail on that minimal pair. Second, the CLUE-style NLU tasks are evaluated by task-specific fine-tuning for sequence classification or multiple-choice classification. Third, the cognitive modeling tasks evaluate whether model representations can predict human fMRI responses: model hidden states are used as features in an encoding model, and the predicted neural responses are compared with observed fMRI responses using correlation-based metrics. 

## 2.1 Natural Language Understanding Track 

The NLU track measures syntactic and semantic understanding in Chinese. It contains two zero-shot minimal-pair tasks and six supervised fine-tuning tasks. The minimal-pair tasks are intended to test whether a pretrained model assigns higher probability or lower pseudo-perplexity to the more acceptable member of a pair without task-specific training. The fine-tuning tasks test whether the representations learned under the BabyLM data budget transfer to standard Chinese understanding tasks. 

ZhoBLiMP. ZhoBLiMP (Liu et al., 2026) is the open zero-shot minimal-pair benchmark in the NLU track. The final evaluation files contain 118 paradigms with 300 examples each, for a total of 35,400 minimal pairs. Each item contrasts an acceptable Chinese sentence with a minimally different unacceptable sentence. The paradigms cover a broad range of Chinese syntactic and semantic phenomena, including ba and bei constructions, classifier–noun agreement, anaphora and binding, argument structure, passive constructions, negative and free-choice polarity items, A-not-A and particle questions, relativization, topicalization, quantification, word order, and modal or raising constructions. Performance on ZhoBLiMP is computed as the proportion of pairs for which the model prefers the acceptable sentence. 

XCOMPS-ZH (Hidden). XCOMPS-ZH is the hidden zero-shot minimal-pair task in the NLU track (He et al., 2025). It contains 14,368 minimal pairs targeting concept–property compatibility. In each pair, the model must prefer a semantically compatible concept–property combination over a minimally altered incompatible one. This task complements ZhoBLiMP by emphasizing commonsense-like semantic compatibility rather than primarily morphosyntactic acceptability. 

AFQMC. AFQMC is a sentence-pair semantic matching task from CLUE (Xu et al., 2020). Given two Chinese sentences, typically short user queries, the model predicts whether they express the same meaning. The final evaluation files contain 34,334 training records and 4,316 development records. This task evaluates paraphrase recognition and robust sentence-level semantic comparison. 

OCNLI. OCNLI is a Chinese natural language inference task (Hu et al., 2020). Each example consists of a premise and a hypothesis, and the model predicts whether the hypothesis is entailed by, contradicts, or is neutral with respect to the premise. The final evaluation files contain 50,437 training records and 2,950 development records. OCNLI evaluates sentence-pair reasoning, lexical semantics, and compositional inference. 

TNEWS. TNEWS is a short-text classification task based on Chinese news titles (Xu et al., 2020). The model predicts the news category from a short input text. The final evaluation files contain 53,360 training records and 10,000 development records. CLUEWSC2020. CLUEWSC2020 is a Chinese Winograd-style coreference task (Xu et al., 2020). Given a sentence, a pronoun, and a candidate antecedent, the model predicts whether the two expressions co-refer. The final evaluation files contain 1,244 training records and 304 development records. This task targets discourse-level reasoning, anaphora resolution, and sensitivity to semantic cues that determine reference. 

C3. C3 is the hidden machine reading comprehension task in the NLU track (Sun et al., 2020). It is a multiple-choice reading comprehension benchmark in which the model answers questions about Chinese passages or dialogues. The final evaluation files contain 11,869 training records and 3,816 development records. Compared with single-sentence or sentence-pair classification tasks, C3 places greater emphasis on integrating information across a passage and selecting the best answer from several candidates. 

Diagnostic NLI (Hidden). The diagnostic nli task is a hidden diagnostic natural language inference task (Hu et al., 2021). It follows the NLI format but is designed for final held-out diagnosis of model inference behavior. The final evaluation files contain 50,437 training records and 2,122 development records. This task is used to probe whether models trained under the ChineseBabyLM data budget learn generalizable inference patterns beyond the open development tasks. 32.2 Cognitive Modeling Track 

The Cognitive Modeling track evaluates whether model representations align with human brain responses during Chinese language comprehension. The two tasks are derived from the Chinese fMRI components of MulCogBench (Zhang et al., 2025). In both tasks, the evaluation pipeline extracts model representations and fits an encoding model from those representations to fMRI responses. Better prediction performance indicates that the semantic information encoded in the model’s representations is more similar to that encoded in the brain’s neural representations elicited by the corresponding linguistic stimuli (Zhang et al., 2024). 

Word fMRI. The word fmri task is a word-level brain-alignment benchmark based on a Chinese word fMRI dataset introduced by Wang et al. (2022b). The dataset contains neural responses from 11 participants as they read 672 isolated Chinese words. The task evaluates whether representations of individual words encode information predictive of the spatial patterns of fMRI responses elicited during word comprehension. This task therefore probes relatively local lexical and semantic representations. 

Discourse fMRI. The fmri task is a discourse-level brain-alignment benchmark based on a Chinese discourse fMRI dataset introduced by Wang et al. (2022a). The dataset contains neural responses from 12 native Chinese speakers as they listened to 60 stories comprising 52,269 words in total. Compared with the word-level task, this task evaluates representations in a more natural and context-rich comprehension setting. It tests whether models encode information useful for predicting neural activity during extended spoken discourse, where lexical, syntactic, semantic, and discourse-contextual information must be integrated over time. 

## 2.3 Hanzi Track 

The Hanzi track evaluates knowledge of properties specific to the Chinese writing system. Unlike the CLUE tasks, which focus on sentence- or passage-level understanding, the Hanzi tasks test whether a model has learned character-level regularities from limited pretraining data. All four Hanzi tasks are evaluated as zero-shot minimal-pair tasks. Each file contains 2,000 minimal pairs. For the two Hanzi structure tasks, target characters are drawn from the Level-1 list of the General Standard Chinese Character Table (Ministry of Education and State Language Commission of China, 2013). Character structures and component decompositions follow the Chinese Character Dataset of Wu et al. (2025). Phonetic-role and IDS-based annotations use ids-analysis.txt from CJKVI-IDS (Kawabata et al., 2018). Target character components must appear at least 50 times in the official pretraining corpus (Section 3.1). For Hanzi pinyin tasks, target characters are restricted to single-pronunciation (monophonic) characters drawn from the 3,500 most frequent Chinese characters, so that every item has an unambiguous reading. Pinyin readings are obtained with pypinyin (manually corrected where mislabeled) and mapped to IPA with Dragonmapper , and each syllable is decomposed into three independent dimensions: the initial (onset consonant), the final (rime), and the lexical tone (1–4). All pinyin items take the form of homophone judgments: each minimal pair contrasts a true statement that two characters share an identical reading with a minimally different false statement in which the second character is replaced by one that differs in exactly one of the three dimensions, yielding the initial diff , rhyme diff , and tone diff conditions. So that the judgment reflects phonological knowledge rather than lexical frequency, the two characters in each pair are matched on corpus frequency via Zipf scores. 

Hanzi structure. The hanzi structure task is the open character-structure benchmark. It evaluates whether a model captures regularities in the internal composition of Chinese characters, including component structure and visually or orthographically motivated constraints. Each file contains two subtypes of minimal pairs: whole-to-component judgments (e.g., whether a stated component occupies the correct position in a target character) and component-to-whole judgments (e.g., which character matches given component descriptions). Items are restricted to five compositional structure types. The model succeeds when it assigns a higher score to the structurally appropriate member of a minimal pair. 

Hidden Hanzi structure. The hanzi structure hidden task is the held-out counterpart of the open Hanzi-structure task. It tests the same broad type of character-structural knowledge but uses hidden final-evaluation items, reducing the risk that systems overfit to the open Hanzi examples. 

Hanzi pinyin. The hanzi pinyin task is a character-phonology benchmark. It evaluates whether a model has learned regularities relating Chinese characters to their pronunciations, represented through pinyin. # 3 Rules for model training 

All participating groups are required to pretrain their models from scratch (that is, randomly initialized weights). Loading pretrained checkpoints or distilling from existing large models is not allowed. 

## 3.1 Pretraining data 

There are two options for pretraining data. Option 1 is to use the official corpus released by the organizers, a 102M-word training corpus sampled from the BabyBabelLM project (Jumelet et al., 2026) 1. Option 2 is to use a self-compiled pretraining corpus, which should contain at most 102M words counted via Jieba 0.42.1 in default mode 2. There is no restriction on the number of training epochs. 

## 3.2 Tokenizer and model architecture 

We do not have any special requirements on the tokenizer or model architecture. Note that the two Hanzi tracks are designed specifically to encourage innovations on tokenizer design so that the models encode natively the phonological and orthographical information of Chinese characters. 

# 4 Baseline models 

We adopted six models as baselines for the first ChineseBabyLM challenge. In addition to four models pretrained from scratch, we evaluated Qwen3-0.6B 3 and BERT-base-Chinese 4 as two established open-source models trained on large-scale corpora. The implementation details of the baselines are as follows. 

Tokenizer We randomly sample roughly 0.8B characters on CCI3-HQ (Wang et al., 2024), a high-quality Chinese pretraining corpus, and trained a byte-level BPE tokenizer with a 50 ,000 vocabulary size and a minimum frequency threshold of 2. The tokenizer was used across all the baseline models. 

Architecture We train four architecture-diverse baseline models: an encoder-decoder Transformer following T5 (Raf-fel et al., 2020), a bidirectional encoder following BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), a selective state-space model following Mamba (Gu and Dao, 2023), and a decoder-only Transformer following Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023). # 6 Future work 

Future ChineseBabyLM challenges can extend the current monolingual text-only setting by following recent updates in the broader BabyLM program while keeping the focus on Chinese-specific learning questions. A multimodal track could combine developmentally plausible Chinese text with image–text data, picture-book pages, instructional diagrams, cap-tions, or OCR-rich scenes, connecting ChineseBabyLM to the multimodal direction explored in prior BabyLM editions (Hu et al., 2024) and testing whether visual grounding improves Hanzi structure, pinyin, and character-level representations. An interaction track could evaluate models that learn from constrained feedback, generated examples, demonstrations, or curriculum-like data selection, following the BabyLM interaction setting (Charpentier et al., 2025); for Chinese, such feedback could target word segmentation, homophone choice, pinyin, character components, classifier use, and discourse coherence, while counting all generated text or feedback within the data budget. Multilingual or Chinese–English bilin-gual tracks could build on BabyBabelLM’s multilingual data setting (Jumelet et al., 2026) to study data mixture ratios, tokenizer sharing, cross-lingual transfer, code-switching, and the effect of joint training on typologically distant languages. Future versions should also incorporate a wider range of Chinese tasks beyond the present NLU, Hanzi, and cognitive modeling tracks, including word segmentation, syntactic parsing, discourse coherence, dialogue, summarization, question answering, educational reasoning, literary or classical Chinese, dialectal variation, and culturally grounded commonsense. As the task expands, clear data accounting and hidden final-evaluation sets will remain important for preserving the BabyLM emphasis on genuinely data-efficient learning (Warstadt et al., 2023a). ChineseBabyLM should also deepen its connection with researchers in language acquisition, second language acqui-sition, Chinese linguistics, psycholinguistics, and education. Beyond organizing shared tasks, future editions can host workshops, tutorials, and invited talks that bring together NLP researchers, acquisition researchers, teachers, and corpus builders to study how Chinese is learned from limited input. This broader community can help define more developmen-tally grounded data sources, propose acquisition-inspired evaluation tasks, compare first- and second-language learning trajectories, and identify phenomena that are central to Chinese learning but underrepresented in current NLP bench-marks. In this way, ChineseBabyLM can serve not only as a competition, but also as an interdisciplinary platform for research on data-efficient Chinese language learning, bilingual acquisition, and cognitively plausible language modeling.
